Om symbol is a sacred sound and symbol. It represents the ultimate reality and the oneness of all things. The symbol’s three curves represent the waking, the dream, and the deep sleep state. Further, the dot represents the fourth state of consciousness.

Spirituality is a worldview that suggests there is a dimension to life that goes beyond what we experience physically and through our senses. It can involve religious or cultural practices and beliefs, a connection with others and the world, and self-improvement. It can also be used in a non-religious sense to refer to a preoccupation with or capacity to understand fundamental moral, existential, or metaphysical questions.

Hinduism, also known as Sanatana Dharma, is the world’s oldest living religion. Sanātana Dharma is a Sanskrit term that can be translated as "eternal religion" or "eternal law". It can also mean "unshakeable, venerable order" or "ancient and continuing guideline". It is a natural religion, meaning its philosophies and practices are considered universally accessible through sincere study, reason, and experience apart from special revelation. Hinduism is also an indigenous religion made up of a diverse family of philosophies and traditions that have been practiced primarily throughout Asia for thousands of years. Today, Hinduism is a global religion with adherents representing virtually every racial, ethnic, and national background and living on every continent, and comprising majorities in three countries:
India, Nepal, and Mauritius.

The six Astika schools of Hindu philosophy, which recognise the authority of the Vedas, are: Sankhya, Yoga, Nyaya, Vaisheshika, Mimamsa, and Vedanta. Hindu texts have been classified into Sruti ("heard") and Smrti ("remembered"). The major Hindu scriptures are the Vedas, the Upanishads, the Purānas, the Mahabharata, the Ramayana, and the Agamas. Prominent themes in Hindu beliefs include karma (action, intent and consequences) and the four Puruṣārthas, proper goals or aims of human life, namely:
1) Dharma (ethics/duties)
2) Artha (prosperity/work)
3) Kama (desires/passions)
4) Moksha (liberation/freedom from the passions and the cycle of death and rebirth).
Hindu religious practices include devotion (bhakti), worship (puja), sacrificial rites (yajña), and meditation (dhyāna) and yoga. The use of the term Sanātana Dharma for Hinduism is a modern usage, based on the belief that the origins of Hinduism lie beyond human history, as revealed in the Hindu texts. Sanātana Dharma refers to "timeless, eternal set of truths" and this is how Hindus view the origins of their religion.

Brahma:

Role:The Creator
Description:Brahma is responsible for the creation of the universe and all living beings. He is often depicted with four heads, each facing a different direction, symbolizing his ability to see and create in all directions.
Consort: Saraswati, the goddess of knowledge, music, arts, and wisdom.

Vishnu:

Role: The Preserver
Description: Vishnu is tasked with the preservation and protection of the universe. He is known for his ten avatars (incarnations), which include well-known forms like Rama and Krishna, through which he descends to restore cosmic order.
Consort: Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth, fortune, and prosperity.

Shiva:

Role: The Destroyer
Description: Shiva is responsible for the destruction and transformation of the universe, which is a necessary part of the cycle of creation and renewal. He is often depicted in a meditative pose or as Nataraja, the cosmic dancer.
Consort: Parvati, who is also revered in her forms as Durga and Kali.

Saraswati:
Goddess of Knowledge, Music, Arts, Wisdom, and Learning Saraswati is depicted as a beautiful woman dressed in white, symbolizing purity and wisdom. She is often shown playing the veena (a stringed musical instrument) and is associated with the swan, symbolizing wisdom and grace. Saraswati is worshipped by students, scholars, artists, and musicians. Saraswati represents the intellectual and creative aspects of the universe.
Lakshmi:
Goddess of Wealth, Fortune, and Prosperity. Lakshmi is depicted as a beautiful woman standing or sitting on a lotus flower, symbolizing purity and spiritual power. She is often shown with four hands, holding lotus flowers, and gold coins flowing from one hand, signifying abundance and prosperity. Elephants, symbols of royal power, often accompany her. Lakshmi represents the intellectual and creative aspects of the universe.
Parvati:
Goddess of Love, Fertility, and Devotion; also known as the Mother Goddess. Parvati is depicted as a nurturing and benevolent mother figure, often shown with two hands and a serene expression. She has many forms, including Durga (the warrior goddess) and Kali (the fierce destroyer of evil), each representing different aspects of her power and divinity. Parvati represents love, devotion, and the nurturing aspects of existence. She is worshipped for family well-being, marital happiness, and protection from evil.
Hinduism also advances the concept of the equal worth of all mankind, as expressed in the ancient hymn:
Ajyesthaso akanishthaso ete sambhrataro vahaduhu saubhagaya.
That means: “No one is superior, none inferior. All are brothers marching forward to prosperity.”

The popularly recited Hindu invocation demonstrates this concern for universal kinship and well-being:
Om sarve bhavantu sukhinah. Sarve santu niraamayaah. Sarve bhadraani pashyantu. Maa kaschid dukhbhaag bhavet.
That means: “May all beings be happy. May all beings be healthy. May all beings experience prosperity. May none in the world suffer.”

Meditation, a process that calms and focuses the psyche, is integral to yogic practice and recognized with yoga for its salutary effects on personal well-being.
